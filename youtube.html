YouTube as it Existed

In February of 2005, three Paypal employees, Chad Hurley, Steve Chen, and Jawed Karim, activated the Youtube domain name. They came up with the idea for Youtube in 2004 when they realized there was no singular website to upload videos globally. The first youtube video uploaded was by Jawed Karim in April 2005 titled “Me At the Zoo.” Youtube was initially prone to many users uploading copyrighted content and content that was inappropriate by today’s Youtube standard.

As seen in the 2005 snapshot of the homepage, Youtube was centered around the user and advertisements were not yet integrated into the design of the homepage. By July 2006, Youtube videos averaged 100 million views per day and approximately 65,000 videos were uploaded daily. The snapshot of the 2006 Youtube home page only continued to add to user focused functions with the integration of a “My” tool bar that had features such as “My Subscriptions”, “My Friends”, etc.

Despite its exponential growth going into 2006, Youtube’s greatest obstacle was making the platform profitable. There was no shortage of users on the Youtube. The problem was original content. A good proportion of Youtube’s content was unlawfully used and lacked original content creators. The beginning of that solution came with Google’s acquisition of Youtube for 1.65 billion dollars. Google saw Youtube as a platform with a large audience and untapped potential for advertisers.

Introduction to Monetization

Google’s main focus after first acquiring Youtube was to grow the user community, which is crazy to think about since by 2007, Youtube had consumed enough internet bandwidth than the entire internet had in the year 2000. It took a little more than year for Google to use Youtube as the cash cow they knew it could be.

In August of 2007, in-video advertisements were introduced into Youtube videos. This had been the first introduction of advertisements that were directly visible on the website. A few months later, Youtube launched the Youtube Partner Program. The Youtube Partner Program is the program that allows users to make money of their content. The key to that sentence is their content. This incentivized users who uploaded videos to ditch uploading copyrighted content and make content of their own. To complement the new monetization program, Youtube also launched Youtube Insights, which provided data on Youtube videos. The beginning of the program provided basic analytics of videos, but we will discuss this tool later.

In 2008, Youtube launched two important features directly affecting monetization: promoted videos and pre-roll ads. Sponsored videos or promoted videos, as they were called, were paid advertisements that would appear when users searched videos using certain key words. It’s the same system Google used to put advertisements after users use Google search. Pre roll ads were also introduced as almost a safeguard if promoted videos were ignored. Pre-roll ads would be played right before the content the user clicked on. The integration of these advertisements lead to Youtube increasing the amount of ad formats on Youtube from one to seven. This integration ads also didn’t deter users, who by October 2008, were uploading 15 hours of content per minute.

Design Changes Post-Monetization

As we highlighted earlier, Youtube’s initial design was totally user focused. Every functionality of Youtube was for the user. This quickly changed with the implementation of advertisement on Youtube.

As can be seen in this design, promoted videos were placed above the featured videos section. The featured videos section was more user based since the videos would be shown if they were trending in popularity. The size of thumbnails in this new 2008 layout also are made smaller to accommodate the new sections of advertised videos. This was a major shift for Youtube and the changes in design only became more prevalent as advertisements become more familiar with Youtube.

Take a look at this screenshot of Youtube from 2010. One thing that is noticeable about this design is the categories that the videos are put in. You see “Entertainment”, “Music”, “News & Politics”, etc. The reason for this categorization may seem user-centric, but advertisers were the real beneficiaries. They needed videos to be categorized so they could tell Youtube where to place there pre-roll advertisements. You will see the “Recommended For You” section of the homepage. This subsection of the homepage signifies the diminishing of user focused initiative on behalf of Youtube and how the placement of a system to generate revenue would be more important to Youtube than it’s users.

Data Mining and Monetization

It was mentioned previously Youtube put into place the Youtube Insights tool into the website. This tool may seem to be useful to the user, but again, the real beneficiary of this analytical tool is Youtube and as the website would continue to grow in popularity and convention, more and more data was collected on Youtube’s users and their behaviors.

The main metric that was used to show content on the homepage was view count. It was easy to use but it suffered from a major problem: clickbait. A catchy title could be shared from user to user, but could be a major spread of misinformation or distasteful content. To combat this problem, Youtube was able to collect other data to influence it’s algorithm such as session-time (how long a user has been on the Youtube platform as a content creator),video duration,how well your video’s metadata (title, description, keywords) fit the user’s search query, and how much your video has engaged users already (likes, comments, watch time). While this data was helpful to advertisers because they know what audiences would watch their advertisements, this put a strain on content creators whose videos were being monetized to put out content on a continual basis so as not to “fall off”.

In 2018, Youtube made the ultimate appeasement to advertisers by implementing the new and now notorious monetization policy that defunded a lot of original content creators on Youtube. The reasoning behind this was so Youtube was now free to focus on much fewer channels who were eligible to be monetized and allowing advertisers to put more confidence in the kinds of channels they associated with their brands. It was later reported in 2018 that these brands who were in favor of the new monetization policy were having their ads placed in videos that promoted fringe content and hate speech.

Consequences of Monetization

As we have outlined, Youtube made clear design changes that were a result of Google’s quest to capitalize on Youtube’s financial potential. However, these consequences had a major impact on the original motivation for creating Youtube: content.

Before the heavy prevalence of advertisements, it was easier for Youtubers to have their videos monetized. It didn’t take as long for up and coming Youtubers to have their content monetized and there are quite a large number of Youtubers now who make a sustainable income solely of Youtube. With the new monetization policies, new Youtube content creators have a more difficult time using the income from monetization from Youtube to sustain them will they create new and original content. Instead of requiring 10,000 lifetime views to be eligible for monetization alone, Youtubers now need 4,000 hours of watch time on their videos in the past 12 months and 1,000 subscribers. This makes it more challenging for new Youtubers because getting subscribers without a strong base is hard and 4,000 hours of watch time in 12 months requires content to be a combination of long, interesting, and shared.

The result of the new monetization policy goes beyond design changes. Youtube may not realize it, but it is holding back future content creators that could make Youtube more money than they could imagine. Youtubers who are just getting on their feet don’t have all this time to make videos without receiving some assistance from the platform for initial efforts.

ORIGINAL YOUTUBE

Cats!

Long Boi

Short videos about animals were very popular.They were low-risk, universally liked, and easy to produce. In early YouTube, only views mattered, not run time, so creators made videos as short at possible.

AdSense was used, which did not play before the video, so viewers could get straight to the content. The lack of advertisement meant that it was not yet profitable to build a career on YouTube.

YouTube was simply about sharing everyday life experiences.

This is an example of content that is popular now, but would have been impossible to promote in YouTube’s early existence. This is for a multitude of reasons.

First, YouTube was not intended for long format videos (it’s limit was capped at 10 minutes). This also meant that, in general, YouTube did not promote videos that were in-depth or meant to spark discussion. There were intended to be light hearted, slice-of-life content.

Second, large companies, like the Daily Show or other news outlets, were not on YouTube. In fact, these companies saw YouTube as a “leech”, stealing and pirating content that was not their own. This resulted in many copyright issues, and YouTube was unable to turn a positive profit until 2009.
